Boycotts

Romer v. Evans

Foubister, Murray. Colorado Ski Areas. Jpg file, 12 Nov. 2012.

While Amendment 2 was looming on the horizon, on July 4th, 1992, Citizens Project, a group inspired by the Amendment 2 legislation, founded as a “group of volunteers who seek to prevent extremists from eliminating our fundamental freedoms”

A successful boycott can help energize the most progressive Coloradans to overturn their new law. And it would send a potent warning to other states that may soon consider similar measures.

Anonomous New York Times Opinion Author

In November, 53 percent of the state’s voters approved Amendment 2, giving Colorado the nickname of “The Hate State”.

A national economic boycott started, with many organizations like the American Civil Liberties Union, the Coalition of Labor Union Women, and others.

Governor Roy Romer and Denver’s mayor Wellington Webb both condemned the amendment. Thousands of protestors were on the streets protesting alongside them. After Amendment 2 was put in place, Colorado estimated that it lost about $40 million in the convention and tourism business.

This was a major hit, as tourism is a major industry in Colorado, mostly from the ski community. Resorts in Aspen put out ads in newspapers, even though they are usually fully booked around Christmastime, stating their opposition to the anti-gay-rights vote. Celebrities like Whoopi Goldberg, Joan Rivers, and Barbra Streisand support the opposition and boycott of Colorado.

The ultimate irony is that you target Colorado's ski industry for the boycott. Twenty-six of the state's 27 ski areas are in counties that voted against Amendment 2. Those areas of the state that heavily supported this law will not be affected by a skiing boycott -- only those people who are working hardest to repeal Amendment 2 will be damaged.

Roger A. Smith, President of the Denver Metro Convention and Visitor's Bureau, Dec. 27. 1992.

One major extreme in the boycott was in New York, where gay rights activists extorted the Celestial Seasonings tea company in Boulder Colorado for $100,000 to be donated to the cause. If they didn’t comply, they would stage a boycott against the company and dump Celestial Tea into New York’s East River, staging a “tea party” as a publicity stunt.

Overall, a Denver Post reporter called Amendment 2 “the biggest public-relations disaster ever to hit Colorado.”
